\textbf{Scope of this note.} This file derives the ideal magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) equations in the conservative form used by the two-dimensional finite-volume solver. Special attention is given to the momentum equation, because this is where fluid inertia, gas pressure, isotropic magnetic pressure, and directional magnetic tension come together. The notation is kept consistent with the MATLAB code and with the conservative-state vector used by the solver.
\end{overviewbox}

\section{Why magnetohydrodynamics is the right model here}
\subsection{From particle motion to fluid fields}
A plasma is a gas made of charged particles. In principle, one could follow every ion and electron individually, but that is usually far too expensive and also unnecessary when the physical scales of interest are much larger than microscopic particle orbits. Magnetohydrodynamics replaces the enormous collection of particles with smooth continuum fields such as density, velocity, pressure, and magnetic field. The central physical assumption is that, on scales large compared with microscopic kinetic lengths, the plasma behaves like a conducting fluid.

\begin{physicsbox}
\textbf{Physical picture.} Ordinary gas dynamics is a fluid pushed by pressure gradients and inertia. MHD is a fluid pushed by pressure gradients, inertia, and magnetic stresses. The magnetic field behaves partly like an isotropic pressure and partly like a tension-bearing elastic structure.
\end{physicsbox}

\subsection{Why the equations are written in conservative form}
The numerical method used in the project is a finite-volume method. A finite-volume method evolves averages of conserved quantities inside computational cells. For that reason, the governing equations are written in a conservative balance-law form: the time change inside a cell equals the net flux through the cell boundaries.

\section{The conserved-state vector}
The solver uses the conserved variable vector
\begin{equation}
\Uvec =
\begin{bmatrix}
\rho \\
\rho u_x \\
\rho u_y \\
\rho u_z \\
E \\
B_x \\
B_y \\
B_z
\end{bmatrix}.
\label{eq:Uvec}
\end{equation}
Each component of \eqref{eq:Uvec} has a direct physical meaning:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.8em]
    \item $\rho$ is the amount of mass per unit volume.
    \item $\rho u_x$, $\rho u_y$, and $\rho u_z$ are momentum densities.
    \item $E$ is the total energy density.
    \item $B_x$, $B_y$, and $B_z$ are stored as part of the state because the magnetic field evolves dynamically.
\end{itemize}

\section{Derivation of the total energy density}
The total energy density in ideal MHD is
\begin{equation}
E = \frac{p}{\gamma-1} + \frac{1}{2}\rho\left(u_x^2+u_y^2+u_z^2\right) + \frac{1}{2}\left(B_x^2+B_y^2+B_z^2\right).
\label{eq:total-energy}
\end{equation}
The three pieces are internal energy, kinetic energy, and magnetic energy. In normalized ideal-MHD units, the magnetic energy density is $\frac12 |\Bvec|^2$.

\section{Derivation of the conservative equations}
The ideal MHD equations used in the solver are
\begin{equation}
\pdv{\Uvec}{t} + \pdv{\Fvec(\Uvec)}{x} + \pdv{\Gvec(\Uvec)}{y} = 0.
\label{eq:cons-law}
\end{equation}
This compact vector equation really contains eight coupled scalar equations. We now explain them one by one.

\subsection{Mass conservation}
Mass conservation states that matter is not created or destroyed inside the fluid element. In differential form,
\begin{equation}
\pdv{\rho}{t} + \nabla\cdot(\rho\uvec)=0.
\label{eq:mass}
\end{equation}
In two-dimensional Cartesian form,
\begin{equation}
\pdv{\rho}{t} + \pdv{}{x}(\rho u_x) + \pdv{}{y}(\rho u_y)=0.
\label{eq:mass2d}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Momentum conservation and the role of magnetic stress}
If there were no magnetic field, the momentum equation would be the Euler equation of compressible gas dynamics. MHD adds the Lorentz force density $\Jvec\times\Bvec$.

\subsubsection{Step 1: Euler equation of compressible gas dynamics}
For a neutral compressible fluid with no magnetic field, Newton's second law per unit volume gives
\begin{equation}
\rho\,\frac{D\uvec}{Dt} = -\nabla p,
\label{eq:euler-lagrangian}
\end{equation}
where $D/Dt = \partial/\partial t + \uvec\cdot\nabla$ is the material derivative. Equation \eqref{eq:euler-lagrangian} says: the fluid accelerates when pressure is higher on one side than the other.

To convert this into conservative form, use the continuity equation. The standard identity is
\begin{equation}
\pdv{}{t}(\rho\uvec) + \nabla\cdot(\rho\uvec\uvec)
= \rho\,\frac{D\uvec}{Dt}.
\label{eq:mom-identity}
\end{equation}
Substituting \eqref{eq:euler-lagrangian} into \eqref{eq:mom-identity} gives the compressible Euler momentum equation,
\begin{equation}
\pdv{}{t}(\rho\uvec) + \nabla\cdot(\rho\uvec\uvec) = -\nabla p.
\label{eq:euler-precons}
\end{equation}
Now rewrite $-\nabla p$ as the divergence of an isotropic stress tensor:
\begin{equation}
-\nabla p = -\nabla\cdot(p\Iten),
\label{eq:pressure-tensor-form}
\end{equation}
because $\nabla\cdot(p\Iten)$ has components $\partial_j(p\delta_{ij}) = \partial_i p$. Therefore,
\begin{equation}
\pdv{}{t}(\rho\uvec) + \nabla\cdot\left[\rho\uvec\uvec + p\Iten\right] = 0.
\label{eq:euler-cons}
\end{equation}
Equation \eqref{eq:euler-cons} is the Euler equation of compressible gas dynamics written as a conservation law.

\subsubsection{Step 2: add the Lorentz force}
In MHD the fluid is electrically conducting, so it feels the magnetic force density
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{f}_{B}=\Jvec\times\Bvec.
\label{eq:lorentz-density}
\end{equation}
The momentum equation becomes
\begin{equation}
\pdv{}{t}(\rho\uvec) + \nabla\cdot\left[\rho\uvec\uvec + p\Iten\right] = \Jvec\times\Bvec.
\label{eq:mhd-mom-with-lorentz}
\end{equation}
To put this in conservative form, we want to rewrite $\Jvec\times\Bvec$ as the divergence of a tensor.

\subsubsection{Step 3: express $\Jvec\times\Bvec$ in terms of $\Bvec$}
In the normalized units used by ideal MHD, Ampere's law is
\begin{equation}
\Jvec = \nabla\times\Bvec.
\label{eq:ampere}
\end{equation}
Therefore,
\begin{equation}
\Jvec\times\Bvec = (\nabla\times\Bvec)\times\Bvec.
\label{eq:lorentz-as-B}
\end{equation}
Now use the vector identity
\begin{equation}
(\nabla\times\Bvec)\times\Bvec
= (\Bvec\cdot\nabla)\Bvec - \nabla\left(\frac{1}{2}|\Bvec|^2\right) + \Bvec(\nabla\cdot\Bvec).
\label{eq:lorentz-identity}
\end{equation}
In ideal MHD we impose the divergence-free constraint
\begin{equation}
\nabla\cdot\Bvec = 0,
\label{eq:divB0}
\end{equation}
so \eqref{eq:lorentz-identity} reduces to
\begin{equation}
\Jvec\times\Bvec = (\Bvec\cdot\nabla)\Bvec - \nabla\left(\frac{1}{2}|\Bvec|^2\right).
\label{eq:lorentz-pressure-tension}
\end{equation}
This is the most important physical decomposition in the momentum equation.

\begin{physicsbox}
\textbf{Meaning of the two magnetic terms.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.8em]
    \item $-\nabla(\frac12 |\Bvec|^2)$ is an \textbf{isotropic magnetic-pressure force}. It pushes away from regions where magnetic-field strength is large.
    \item $(\Bvec\cdot\nabla)\Bvec$ is a \textbf{directional magnetic-tension force}. It acts along field lines and tends to straighten them when they are bent.
\end{itemize}
So magnetic stress is not purely pressure-like. It has both an isotropic part and an anisotropic part.
\end{physicsbox}

\subsubsection{Step 4: write the magnetic force as a divergence}
We now show how \eqref{eq:lorentz-pressure-tension} becomes a flux divergence. First note that
\begin{equation}
\nabla\cdot\left(\frac12 |\Bvec|^2\Iten\right) = \nabla\left(\frac12 |\Bvec|^2\right).
\label{eq:isotropic-div}
\end{equation}
Next consider the dyadic tensor $\Bvec\Bvec$, whose divergence has components
\begin{equation}
\left[\nabla\cdot(\Bvec\Bvec)\right]_i
= \partial_j(B_i B_j)
= B_j\partial_j B_i + B_i\partial_j B_j.
\label{eq:BB-div-components}
\end{equation}
Using index notation, this is
\begin{equation}
\nabla\cdot(\Bvec\Bvec) = (\Bvec\cdot\nabla)\Bvec + \Bvec(\nabla\cdot\Bvec).
\label{eq:BB-div}
\end{equation}
When $\nabla\cdot\Bvec=0$, this simplifies to
\begin{equation}
\nabla\cdot(\Bvec\Bvec) = (\Bvec\cdot\nabla)\Bvec.
\label{eq:BB-div-simplified}
\end{equation}
Substitute \eqref{eq:isotropic-div} and \eqref{eq:BB-div-simplified} into \eqref{eq:lorentz-pressure-tension}. Then
\begin{equation}
\Jvec\times\Bvec
= -\nabla\cdot\left[\frac12 |\Bvec|^2\Iten - \Bvec\Bvec\right].
\label{eq:lorentz-stress-form}
\end{equation}
This tensor is the magnetic part of the stress tensor.

\subsubsection{Step 5: final conservative momentum equation}
Insert \eqref{eq:lorentz-stress-form} into \eqref{eq:mhd-mom-with-lorentz}. The result is
\begin{equation}
\pdv{}{t}(\rho\uvec)
+ \nabla\cdot\left[\rho\uvec\uvec + p\Iten + \frac12 |\Bvec|^2\Iten - \Bvec\Bvec\right] = 0.
\label{eq:momentum-expanded}
\end{equation}
Grouping the two isotropic pieces gives
\begin{equation}
\pdv{}{t}(\rho\uvec)
+ \nabla\cdot\left[\rho\uvec\uvec + \left(p+\frac12 |\Bvec|^2\right)\Iten - \Bvec\Bvec\right] = 0.
\label{eq:momentum-tensor}
\end{equation}
Equation \eqref{eq:momentum-tensor} is the corrected form used by the solver. The quantity inside the time derivative is the \textbf{momentum density} $\rho\uvec$, not just the velocity $\uvec$.

\subsubsection{Component view in two dimensions}
For the $x$-momentum equation,
\begin{equation}
\pdv{}{t}(\rho u_x)
+ \pdv{}{x}\left(\rho u_x^2 + p + \frac12 |\Bvec|^2 - B_x^2\right)
+ \pdv{}{y}\left(\rho u_x u_y - B_x B_y\right) = 0.
\label{eq:xmom2d}
\end{equation}
For the $y$-momentum equation,
\begin{equation}
\pdv{}{t}(\rho u_y)
+ \pdv{}{x}\left(\rho u_y u_x - B_y B_x\right)
+ \pdv{}{y}\left(\rho u_y^2 + p + \frac12 |\Bvec|^2 - B_y^2\right) = 0.
\label{eq:ymom2d}
\end{equation}
These are exactly the forms that later appear inside the flux vectors used by the MATLAB code.

\subsection{Energy conservation}
The energy equation in conservative form is
\begin{equation}
\pdv{E}{t} + \nabla\cdot\left[\left(E+\ptot\right)\uvec - (\uvec\cdot\Bvec)\Bvec\right]=0,
\label{eq:energy-cons}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\ptot = p + \frac{1}{2}|\Bvec|^2.
\label{eq:ptot}
\end{equation}
The first term inside the energy flux, $(E+\ptot)\uvec$, resembles the usual fluid-energy advection term but with total pressure instead of only gas pressure. The second term, $(\uvec\cdot\Bvec)\Bvec$, accounts for electromagnetic energy transport along the magnetic field.

\subsection{Induction equation}
The induction equation for ideal MHD is
\begin{equation}
\pdv{\Bvec}{t} - \nabla\times(\uvec\times\Bvec)=0.
\label{eq:induction-curl}
\end{equation}
Using vector identities and the divergence-free condition $\nabla\cdot\Bvec=0$, this can be written in conservative component form.

\subsection{Divergence-free constraint}
An additional constraint must hold:
\begin{equation}
\nabla\cdot\Bvec = 0.
\label{eq:divB-zero}
\end{equation}
In two dimensions this becomes
\begin{equation}
\pdv{B_x}{x} + \pdv{B_y}{y} = 0.
\label{eq:divB-2d}
\end{equation}
This is not an optional detail. It expresses the empirical absence of magnetic monopoles. Numerically, if this constraint is violated too strongly, the solver can produce unphysical forces.

\section{Flux vectors used by the MATLAB code}
Using the definitions above, the $x$-direction flux vector is
\begin{equation}
\Fvec =
\begin{bmatrix}
\rho u_x \\
\rho u_x^2 + \ptot - B_x^2 \\
\rho u_x u_y - B_x B_y \\
\rho u_x u_z - B_x B_z \\
(E+\ptot)u_x - (\uvec\cdot\Bvec)B_x \\
0 \\
u_x B_y - u_y B_x \\
u_x B_z - u_z B_x
\end{bmatrix}.
\label{eq:Fx}
\end{equation}
The $y$-direction flux vector is
\begin{equation}
\Gvec =
\begin{bmatrix}
\rho u_y \\
\rho u_y u_x - B_y B_x \\
\rho u_y^2 + \ptot - B_y^2 \\
\rho u_y u_z - B_y B_z \\
(E+\ptot)u_y - (\uvec\cdot\Bvec)B_y \\
u_y B_x - u_x B_y \\
0 \\
u_y B_z - u_z B_y
\end{bmatrix}.
\label{eq:Gy}
\end{equation}

\section{Summary}
The conservative ideal-MHD momentum equation can look opaque when written as a compact tensor divergence, but its logic is straightforward:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.8em]
    \item start from Newton's second law for a compressible fluid,
    \item write the gas-dynamic part in conservative Euler form,
    \item add the Lorentz force density $\Jvec\times\Bvec$,
    \item use Ampere's law and the identity for $(\nabla\times\Bvec)\times\Bvec$,
    \item separate the magnetic force into isotropic magnetic pressure and directional magnetic tension,
    \item rewrite those terms as the divergence of a stress tensor.
\end{enumerate}
That derivation produces the same conservative momentum fluxes that appear later in the MATLAB implementation.
